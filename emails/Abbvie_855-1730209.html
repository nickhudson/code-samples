Healthier living with Parkinson's

You count on your healthcare team for medical support. But there are some things you can do, too, to make the most of everyday living. Making a few changes to your routine—including exercise, diet, and basic activities—may make a difference in how you live with Parkinson's.
Exercise
Regular exercise may help you maintain your strength, mobility, flexibility, and balance. Before you start anything new, though, speak with your doctor to make sure that 1) he or she knows what activities you are choosing and 2) the exercise is safe.1 You can also talk to a physical therapist who can help get you started on a safe exercise plan that's tailored to your ability.1

Some exercise suggestions:
Walking
Gardening
Dancing
Tai chi
Yoga
Sitting and standing exercise
To reduce stress or tension, some people also find massage or meditation helpful.
Diet
Good nutrition is important at any age. When you have Parkinson's disease, there are a few nutrition guidelines to keep in mind2:
Eat a balanced diet with all of the daily nutritional requirements
Maintain bowel regularity
Adjust nutritional priorities for your situation and stage of disease
Eat enough fresh fruits and vegetables—they can provide fiber that will help or prevent constipation
Drink plenty of fluids during the day to keep hydrated. Dehydration can add to fatigue
If you need help meeting any of these goals, talk to your doctor or a nutritionist.

Your motor symptoms may make eating or drinking difficult. But don't do without—there are many solutions out there:
Use weighted drinking glasses, which may be easier to handle3
Avoid drinking from bottles; cups with lids and straws may be easier3
Try plastic food containers to avoid breaks4
Home improvement
Because Parkinson's may affect balance, you may be more likely to trip or fall in your home.5 Changing your home environment may give you more safe space for daily activities. You can “Parkinson-proof” your home by:
Removing small rugs4
Installing “grab bars” in bathrooms (particularly in showers), and adding a shower chair4
Using longer handles (rather than knobs) on kitchen cupboards6
Adjusting your bed to the right height7
Rearranging furniture to create more room8
Staying active
Healthy living is also about staying active in areas of life that are important to you. If before your diagnosis you enjoyed activities such as art, community programs, or volunteering, speak with your doctor about how you can continue with these activities.
